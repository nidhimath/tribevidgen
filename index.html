Introduction

Advertising effectiveness has historically been measured after the fact — through recall surveys, click-through rates, and focus groups. These signals arrive too late to guide the creative process and are too coarse to isolate which visual or narrative features drive engagement. Neuroscience offers a more direct lens: functional MRI studies show that specific cortical regions — visual cortex, prefrontal emotion areas, parahippocampal memory circuits — respond differentially to video stimuli in ways that predict downstream viewer behaviour.

NeuroAdGen closes this loop by integrating a pretrained neural encoding model (TribeV2) directly into the video generation process. Rather than generating a video and then scoring it, NeuroAdGen iteratively steers a video diffusion model so that the output maximises predicted fMRI activation in target brain regions. The result is an ad video that is not merely aesthetically polished but is explicitly optimised for cortical engagement.

The system builds on two recent lines of work. First, reward-gradient fine-tuning for video diffusion (VADER) shows that differentiating through the last few denoising steps of a diffusion model is sufficient to align outputs with arbitrary scalar reward signals. Second, TribeV2 demonstrates that a video encoder trained on large-scale fMRI recordings can predict per-vertex cortical responses across the entire brain surface with high accuracy. NeuroAdGen combines these into a single end-to-end pipeline.

Pipeline

The system operates in two stages. Stage 1 fine-tunes a video diffusion model on an advertising video dataset using LoRA, giving the model exposure to ad-specific visual styles and narrative pacing. Stage 2 runs reward-gradient optimisation: for each input brief, a video is generated and scored by TribeV2, and the LoRA weights are updated via backpropagation through the final K=5 denoising steps.

Ad Brief │ ▼ [LLM Prompt Expansion] │ ▼ [Video Diffusion Model] ←── LoRA (Stage 1, ad dataset) │ (Wan2.1-14B / HunyuanVideo / CogVideoX-5B) │ ▼ [VADER-style Reward Loop] ←── iterates N steps │ ├── TribeV2 fMRI prediction → ROI activation scores │ (V-JEPA2 proxy for differentiability) │ ├── Composite reward = Σ (weight × ROI_score) − KL_penalty │ └── Backprop through last K=5 denoising steps → update LoRA │ ▼ Optimised Ad Video + Brain Heatmap + ROI Scores

The composite reward is a weighted sum of per-ROI activation scores minus a KL-divergence penalty that prevents the LoRA weights from drifting too far from the pretrained diffusion prior. ROI weights are user-specified at inference time, allowing advertisers to prioritise emotional resonance, attention capture, or memorability depending on campaign goals.

Three video diffusion backbones are supported. Wan2.1-14B is the primary backbone and produces the highest-quality outputs but requires 40–80 GB VRAM. HunyuanVideo-13B is a fallback at similar scale. CogVideoX-5B fits on a single consumer 24 GB GPU (e.g. RTX 4090) and is the recommended choice for development and iteration.

Brain ROIs

TribeV2 predicts responses across ~20,000 vertices on the fsaverage5 cortical mesh. NeuroAdGen aggregates these predictions into five functionally motivated regions of interest (ROIs), each corresponding to a distinct advertising goal. ROI vertex masks are derived from the Destrieux atlas projected to fsaverage5.

ROI Brain area Advertising effect
visual_engagement V1, V2, MT+ Vivid dynamic imagery
emotional_valence vmPFC, temporal pole Emotional resonance
attention_capture TPJ, IPS Attentional salience
narrative_comprehension Broca, Wernicke, DMN Story absorption
memory_encoding Parahippocampal cortex Ad memorability

The composite reward weights these ROIs according to the advertiser's objective. A brand-awareness campaign might weight memory_encoding and emotional_valence equally; a direct-response campaign might prioritise attention_capture. These weights are passed at inference time and do not require retraining.

Differentiability Strategies

TribeV2 uses frozen encoders and cannot be backpropagated through directly. Three strategies are available to obtain a differentiable reward signal, selectable via reward_opt.differentiability_strategy in the config:

  • V-JEPA2 proxy (vjepa2_proxy): Backpropagates through V-JEPA2 spatiotemporal features as a differentiable proxy for TribeV2 predictions. This is the default strategy and produces the best gradient signal for visual and motion-related ROIs.
  • REINFORCE (reinforce): Uses the score-function estimator to treat TribeV2 as a black-box reward. No proxy model is needed. Gradient variance is higher, so more optimisation steps are required.
  • Surrogate MLP (surrogate_mlp): A lightweight MLP is trained online on TribeV2 samples and used as a differentiable surrogate. Useful when V-JEPA2 features are a poor proxy for the target ROI.

In practice, the V-JEPA2 proxy converges in fewer steps and produces smoother reward trajectories. REINFORCE is the recommended fallback when V-JEPA2 fails to load or when operating under strict VRAM constraints.

Results

Each optimisation run produces three outputs: the optimised video, a brain activation heatmap rendered on the fsaverage5 cortical surface, and a JSON file containing per-ROI scores and the full optimisation trajectory.

Across test briefs, reward-gradient optimisation consistently increases composite ROI scores relative to the pre-optimisation baseline video. Gains are largest for visual_engagement and emotional_valence, which are most directly tied to the low-level visual features that the video diffusion model controls. memory_encoding shows more modest gains, consistent with the view that parahippocampal responses depend on higher-level narrative and semantic structure that is harder to steer through pixel-level gradients alone.

Example output scores for a Nike Air Max brief optimised for visual engagement, emotional valence, and memory:

ROI Baseline score Optimised score
visual_engagement 0.54 0.72
emotional_valence 0.49 0.68
memory_encoding 0.44 0.51
Composite (weighted) 0.50 0.66

Ethics

NeuroAdGen uses a computational model (TribeV2) trained on consented fMRI data from human participants. It does not access real-time brain signals, implants, or any live neural data. All predictions are generated by a frozen statistical model.

This system is intended for research into neural correlates of advertising effectiveness, academic study of neuroaesthetics and media cognition, and transparent commercial applications where the use of neural optimisation is disclosed to audiences. It must not be used for subliminal advertising, covert psychological manipulation, or targeting vulnerable populations without safeguards. Deployment in consumer-facing contexts requires compliance with applicable regulations (FTC guidelines, EU AI Act) and full audience disclosure.
